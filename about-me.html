About

The path to Foltz.ai

My interest in the law didn't come from a courtroom or some fancy internship. I was 15, and I watched someone I cared about get chewed up by a system that was supposed to help them. That was it for me — I wanted to be a public defender. I wanted to stand up for the people that everyone else seemed fine ignoring.

From UCLA to a Turning Point

I worked hard, got into UCLA, and landed a full ride to law school. Things were on track. Then I got arrested for shoplifting. One bad decision, and the scholarship was gone. So was the straight line to becoming a lawyer.

"A single mistake shouldn't define your access to justice, or your ability to contribute to it."

Finding a New Path

Losing that path made me look at the legal system from a completely different angle. I signed up for a legal technology class at my local community college, and that's where Foltz.ai started taking shape. I figured out that even if I wasn't going to be the one standing in the courtroom, I could build tools that actually helped the people who were.

Most of what passes for "legal work" is really just forms, deadlines, and red tape — stuff that keeps people out instead of letting them in. I kept thinking about Clara Shortridge Foltz, the woman who basically invented public defense, and how the right technology could pick up where her work left off.

The Birth of Foltz

Foltz.ai came out of that turning point. It's built on a simple idea: justice should be accessible, private, and powered by tools that work for the user — not some cloud company. My path here wasn't clean, but it gave me a perspective most people in legal tech don't have. I know what it feels like to be on the wrong side of the gap between people and the law. That's the gap we're closing.
